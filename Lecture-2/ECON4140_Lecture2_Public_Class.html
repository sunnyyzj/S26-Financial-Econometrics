3. Scatterplots

A scatterplot is the first tool for checking the relationship between two quantitative variables.

What to Look For

  • Direction: positive or negative
  • Form: linear, curved, clustered
  • Strength: tight or loose pattern
  • Outliers: unusual influential points

Professional wording

“There appears to be a strong negative linear association between x and y.”

“The relationship may be nonlinear, so Pearson correlation alone may be misleading.”

4. Covariance

Covariance describes whether two variables tend to move together, but its magnitude depends on units, so it is not ideal for measuring strength.

Population Covariance

\(\sigma_{XY}=\frac{\sum (x_i-\mu_X)(y_i-\mu_Y)}{N}\)

Sample Covariance

\(s_{xy}=\frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{n-1}\)
Covariance SignInterpretation
\(s_{xy}>0\)x and y tend to move in the same direction
\(s_{xy}<0\)as x increases, y tends to decrease
\(s_{xy}\approx0\)little or no linear co-movement
Important: The magnitude of covariance has no direct meaning because it depends on the units of x and y.

Practice 2 — Interpret Covariance

Suppose \(s_{xy}=-25.31\). Interpret this number professionally.

The negative sign indicates a negative association: as x increases, y tends to decrease. The magnitude 25.31 has no direct interpretation because covariance depends on measurement units.

5. Correlation

Correlation standardizes covariance, removing the effect of units. It measures the direction and strength of a linear relationship.

Population Correlation

\(\rho_{XY}=\frac{\sigma_{XY}}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}\)

Sample Correlation

\(r_{xy}=\frac{s_{xy}}{s_xs_y} =\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}}\)
Value of rInterpretation
\(r\approx1\)strong positive linear relationship
\(r\approx-1\)strong negative linear relationship
\(r\approx0\)weak linear relationship, but not necessarily no relationship
Exam wording: “Correlation near zero only means weak linear relationship. The variables may still have a nonlinear relationship.”

Practice 3 — Multiple Choice

Which statement is correct?

A) Positive linear relationship means all points must lie exactly on a straight line.
B) Stronger positive relationship means r is closer to -1.
C) Two variables that are uncorrelated may still be related nonlinearly.
D) Stronger negative relationship means r is closer to +1.

Correct answer: C. Correlation only measures linear association; nonlinear relationships may have \(r\approx0\).

6. Spearman’s Rank Correlation

Spearman correlation is the Pearson correlation applied to ranks. It is useful when the relationship is monotonic but not necessarily linear, or when raw values contain strong outliers.

\(r_s=\rho_{R(X),R(Y)}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(R(X),R(Y))}{\sigma_{R(X)}\sigma_{R(Y)}}\)
If ranks are distinct: \(\displaystyle r_s=1-\frac{6\sum d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)}\)
Pearson rSpearman \(r_s\)
Uses raw valuesUses ranks
Measures linear associationMeasures monotonic association
Sensitive to outliersOften less sensitive to outliers

Practice 4 — Spearman Formula

For \(n=5\), suppose \(\sum d_i^2=2\). Compute \(r_s\).

\(r_s=1-\frac{6(2)}{5(5^2-1)}=1-\frac{12}{120}=0.90\).

7. Association vs. Causality

Descriptive Question

Asks about patterns in data.

Example: How does household spending on food vary with income?

Research Question

Asks about causal effect.

Example: How much does attendance affect learning?

Key Concepts

ConceptMeaning
Observational DataVariables are naturally observed; many lurking variables may exist.
Experimental DataResearcher randomly assigns x values when possible.
Endogeneityx is associated with unobserved factors that also affect y.
Exogeneityx is not associated with factors that also affect y.
Lurking VariableA hidden factor affecting both x and y.
Core sentence: Correlation does not necessarily imply causation. A strong correlation may be driven by omitted variables, reverse causality, or selection.

8. Practice Bank

Practice 5 — Correlation Interpretation

Suppose \(r=-0.9421\). Interpret both sign and magnitude.

The negative sign means x and y move in opposite directions. The magnitude \(|r|=0.9421\) indicates a very strong linear relationship.

Practice 6 — Strongest Positive Correlation

Given correlations \(r_{wx}=0.3251\), \(r_{wy}=0.1711\), \(r_{wz}=0.6758\), \(r_{xy}=0.1259\), \(r_{xz}=0.4332\), \(r_{yz}=0.1873\), which pair has the strongest positive correlation?

The strongest positive correlation is between w and z, because \(0.6758\) is the largest positive correlation.

Practice 7 — Variance-Covariance Matrix

If \(s_x=3\), \(s_y=4\), and \(r_{xy}=0.5\), find \(s_{xy}\). What are the diagonal elements of the variance-covariance matrix?

\(s_{xy}=r_{xy}s_xs_y=0.5(3)(4)=6\). Diagonal elements are variances: \(s_x^2=9\), \(s_y^2=16\). Matrix: \(\begin{pmatrix}9&6\\6&16\end{pmatrix}\).

Practice 8 — Panel Data

Pollution data are observed for 103 water stations across 5 years. What type of data is this, and what is the unit of observation?

This is panel data. The unit of observation is a water station in a particular year. There are \(103\times5=515\) station-year observations before accounting for missing values.

Practice 9 — Endogeneity

Researchers find a positive correlation between long-term PM2.5 exposure and dementia rates. Why should we be careful before making a causal claim?

PM2.5 exposure may be endogenous. People do not randomly choose where to live; income, education, health, smoking, and neighborhood conditions may affect both pollution exposure and dementia risk.
Lecture 2 Takeaway: Use scatterplots to see the pattern, covariance to identify direction, correlation to measure linear strength, and causality concepts to avoid over-interpreting association.
